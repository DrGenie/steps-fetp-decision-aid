What STEPS does

STEPS helps policy makers and partners explore options for scaling Field Epidemiology Training Programs in India. It connects three main inputs and shows how they work together in simple, transparent metrics:

  • Preferences from a discrete choice experiment with key stakeholders.
  • Costing templates from WHO, NIE and NCDC for Frontline, Intermediate and Advanced programmes.
  • Simple epidemiological multipliers that translate training scale up into public health benefits.

Users can set a programme configuration, see endorsement rates, costs and benefit cost ratios, and compare saved scenarios side by side for business case discussions with the World Bank and government counterparts.

Each tab has a short explanation and information icons next to technical terms. Hover or tap the icons for a plain language summary of the concept.

How to use the tool

  1. Start in the Configuration tab and choose programme tier, career incentives, mentorship intensity, delivery mode, response time, cohort size, number of cohorts and cost per trainee per month.
  2. Keep the cost slider within the range used in the experiment (75,000 to 400,000 rupees per trainee per month) so that results remain consistent with the underlying models.
  3. Select the preference model: the overall Average mixed logit model or the Supportive group identified in the latent class analysis.
  4. Click Apply configuration to update all metrics. Click View results summary to open a concise modal summary that can be used in meetings and emails.
  5. Save promising configurations in the Saved scenarios tab and download them as Excel or as a short policy brief PDF.
  6. Use the Advanced and methods tab to adjust epidemiological assumptions and review the technical appendix. These settings are useful during workshops when teams want to align the tool with updated national assumptions.

STEPS is designed for laptops, desktops and tablets. It can be projected during policy discussions or used directly by technical staff in ministries and partner organisations.

Attributes and levels in the FETP preference study

The discrete choice experiment asked stakeholders to choose between alternative FETP program designs and an opt out option. Each program was described by the attributes below. STEPS keeps program configurations inside the experimental range so that results remain consistent with the underlying models and do not extrapolate beyond the evidence.

Program design attributes

Programme tier

Levels used in the experiment

Indicates whether the program is Frontline, Intermediate or Advanced. Duration reflects the typical length of training and field work for each tier, and signals the depth of skills that graduates are expected to acquire.

Career incentive

Levels used in the experiment
Government and partner certificate
University qualification
Government career pathway

Shows the main career benefit for completing the program. Incentives influence how attractive training is for potential trainees and their organisations and can affect recruitment and long term retention in epidemiology roles.

Mentorship intensity

Levels used in the experiment
Low (at least 5 fellows per mentor)
Medium (3 to 4 fellows per mentor)
High (maximum 2 fellows per mentor)

Captures the amount of hands on mentoring support that trainees receive from experienced epidemiologists. Higher intensity mentoring is more attractive and can improve quality, but it also increases programme cost and staff time requirements.

Delivery mode

Levels used in the experiment
Blended (mix of in person, online and field work)
Fully in person
Fully online

Describes how teaching and supervision are delivered. Blended delivery combines classroom, online and field components. Fully online options can reduce some costs and travel demands but may lower endorsement if stakeholders value face to face interaction with mentors and peers.

Expected response time for events

Levels used in the experiment
Detect and respond within 30 days
Detect and respond within 15 days
Detect and respond within 7 days

Summarises how quickly graduates are expected to help the system detect and respond to outbreaks or public health events once training is complete. Faster response times are more attractive but may require more intensive training and support.

Cost per trainee per month

Levels used in the experiment
75,000; 100,000; 250,000; 350,000; 400,000 INR per trainee per month

Total program cost per trainee per month. In the experiment this varied between 75,000 and 400,000 rupees per trainee per month. STEPS keeps the cost slider inside this range so that outputs remain consistent with the values used in the preference models.

Opt out and cost templates

Opt out alternative

Definition
A status quo option where no new FETP training is funded under the scenario being considered.

The discrete choice experiment included an explicit opt out option alongside the training options. STEPS uses this as the benchmark when calculating endorsement. Endorsement is the predicted share choosing the configured FETP option rather than opting out, given the attribute levels and cost.

Cost templates

Definition
Template budgets submitted by WHO, NIE and NCDC for different FETP tiers.

Templates are used as alternative settings rather than being combined. Policy makers can switch between templates to see how cost structure affects total economic cost while holding programme design constant. Templates that include the opportunity cost of trainee time feed into total economic cost when the opportunity cost switch is on.

Opportunity cost of trainee time

Definition
The value of trainee salary time spent in training instead of normal duties, per trainee per month.

When the opportunity cost toggle is on, STEPS adds the value of trainee time to programme cost. This yields total economic cost. When it is off, outputs focus on budgetary cost only. This distinction is important for economic evaluation and for budgeting discussions.

Preference models

Definition
Average mixed logit model and a supportive latent class group identified from a two class model.

Users can switch between the average mixed logit model and the supportive latent class group. Endorsement and willingness to pay values change accordingly. This can be useful for sensitivity analysis, for example comparing overall stakeholder preferences with the views of those most supportive of training investment.

The Attributes tab is intended as a reference during workshops. It summarises what was randomised in the discrete choice experiment and clarifies how the tool links back to those experimental choices.

Cost templates and sources

Cost templates are based on submissions from WHO, NIE and NCDC. For Frontline training, WHO supplies a template for several cohorts. For Intermediate training, templates are available from WHO, NIE and NCDC. For Advanced training, templates are available from NIE and NCDC.

Templates are used as alternative settings rather than being combined. Policy makers can switch between templates to see how cost structure affects economic cost while holding programme design constant.

About STEPS

The University of Newcastle is developing a business case for strengthening and scaling FETP programmes in India. STEPS is one of the tools in this work. It provides a structured way to test scale up options and explain cost and benefit implications in policy discussions.

The tool is designed to support the Government of India, WHO, the World Bank and other partners as they plan investments in epidemiology training. It helps answer questions such as:

  • How do costs and benefits change when moving from Frontline to Intermediate or Advanced tiers?
  • What happens to endorsement when mentorship intensity is increased?
  • How many cohorts and trainees are needed to reach a national scale target?

Prepared by Mesfin Genie, PhD, Newcastle Business School, The University of Newcastle, Australia. For questions or clarifications please contact mesfin.genie@newcastle.edu.au.

Advanced settings and assumptions

These settings control how epidemiological outcomes and monetary benefits are computed. They are pre populated with evidence informed but conservative defaults and can be adjusted during workshops. Changes are recorded in the assumption log and reflected immediately in the Results and National simulation tabs for the current browser session.

Key assumptions include:

  • Time horizon for benefits in years.
  • Outbreak responses per graduate or cohort per year by programme tier.
  • Monetary value of one outbreak response and of one FETP graduate in INR.
  • Exchange rate used for USD display and the currency mode shown in the interface.

Use these settings when you want to align STEPS assumptions with updated national estimates or discussions with partner organisations. For example, you can change the indicative value per graduate or per outbreak response, or match the INR to USD display rate to current assumptions used elsewhere in health security modelling.
